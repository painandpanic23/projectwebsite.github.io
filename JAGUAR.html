JAGUAR

(Panthera onca)

Here is a description of its key physical characteristics:

Size and Build:
Jaguars are stocky, muscular, and compact animals. They have a large head and a broad forehead, with short, powerful limbs and a relatively short tail compared to their body length. They are the third-largest cat in the world, after the tiger and the lion. Adult males typically weigh between 140 and 250 pounds, while females are smaller, weighing between 100 and 180 pounds. Their body length, from nose to the base of the tail, can be from 5 to 6 feet.

Coat and Markings:
The jaguar's coat is its most distinctive feature. It is a pale yellow to reddish-yellow or tan color on top, with a whitish underside on the throat, belly, and legs. The coat is covered in black spots. On the head and neck, these spots are typically solid, but on the sides of the body, they form complex patterns called rosettes. A key difference between a jaguar and a leopard is that the jaguar's rosettes are larger and often contain one or more small dots in the center.

Melanism ("Black Panther")
Like leopards, jaguars can be melanistic, meaning they have a gene that results in an excess of dark pigment. These all-black jaguars are often called "black panthers." While they appear to be solid black, their rosettes are still faintly visible in certain lighting.

Jaw and Bite Force:
The jaguar has an exceptionally strong jaw and is known for having the most powerful bite force of all the big cats. This strength allows it to pierce the skulls of its prey with a single bite, a unique hunting method among felines.

Vocalization:
Unlike cougars, jaguars can roar, a sound they use to communicate with other jaguars, especially during mating season.
